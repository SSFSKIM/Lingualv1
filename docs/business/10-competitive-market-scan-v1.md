## Executive Market Read

The market is already crowded around "AI speaking" and "AI conversation practice." Lingual should not position itself as simply an AI conversation app. The stronger position is:

> Curriculum-controlled speaking practice for schools, with teacher-visible evidence, pilot reporting, and institution-ready onboarding.

Three competitive pressures are clear:

1. **US school SaaS competitors are setting the institutional baseline.**
   Products such as Speakable, Speakology AI, Telo AI, Extempore, ELL Speak, Nualang, ELSA Schools, and FLOW Speak show that buyers may expect assignable speaking tasks, teacher dashboards, feedback, transcripts/recordings, LMS or roster support, privacy claims, and quote/PO-ready sales.

2. **Korea is not an empty market.**
   EBS AI PengTalk, LG CNS AI Tutor / Speaking Class, AI Digital Textbook publishers, and private-institute products already make AI English speaking familiar to schools, parents, and education offices. Lingual's Korea opportunity is not novelty; it is a better school workflow and clearer learning evidence.

3. **College and global language-program competitors validate the same category.**
   Flowent, Talio, LinguaPal, Gliglish, TrySpeak, inqui.ai, Beyond Words, Alelo, and EF Speak show that language programs are actively looking for scalable speaking practice and instructor visibility. College remains a later Lingual sales segment, but these products are strategically useful because they reveal mature B2B expectations.

## Market Categories

1. **Direct school AI conversation-practice platforms.**
   These products are closest to Lingual because they combine speaking/listening practice with school, teacher, or program workflows.

2. **Speaking assessment and assignment platforms.**
   These are close because they solve the speaking-scale problem, but may be more assessment/submission-oriented than realtime conversation-oriented.

3. **Public-sector or textbook-linked AI English systems.**
   This is especially important in Korea, where public education adoption and AI Digital Textbook policy shape the buyer path.

4. **College/language-program AI speaking platforms.**
   These may not match Lingual's first K-12 target, but they are important because they validate language-program demand for scalable speaking practice.

5. **Consumer or private-institute AI English apps.**
   These are not primary competitors unless they sell into schools or institutions, but they are useful as product and pricing references.

## Positioning Implications For Lingual

1. **Do not lead with "AI conversation practice."**
   The phrase is becoming commoditized. Many competitors can already claim realtime speaking, AI feedback, roleplay, or pronunciation support.

2. **Lead with curriculum-controlled speaking practice.**
   The under-served wedge is not only that students can speak with AI, but that teachers can decide what the AI practice is for, connect it to class objectives, and see useful evidence afterward.

3. **Make teacher-visible evidence a core product story.**
   Sales should emphasize outputs that help teachers and schools make decisions: speaking time, average session length, completion, student/teacher satisfaction, sample debriefs, implementation friction, and post-pilot expansion interest.

4. **Treat LMS/rostering/admin as market expectations, not premium differentiation.**
   US school competitors already talk about Canvas, Google Classroom, Clever, ClassLink, LTI, rosters, dashboards, gradebooks, and privacy. Lingual can start with manual setup or light Canvas workflow, but the roadmap should acknowledge this baseline.

5. **For Korea, position against public-sector and textbook-linked incumbents carefully.**
   Plang School appears to understand Korean English teacher assessment workflows; AI PengTalk and LG CNS have public legitimacy; AIDT publishers have distribution. Lingual should position as a flexible speaking-practice layer that can support different curricula, pilots, and evidence reporting rather than as a replacement for official textbook systems.

6. **College products are strategically useful even if college is a later sales segment.**
   Flowent, Talio, and similar products show what mature language programs may request: instructor dashboards, rubric scoring, cohort analytics, privacy documentation, and paid pilot structures.

7. **Speech scoring alone is not defensible.**
   Korea infrastructure vendors and US speaking platforms already offer pronunciation/scoring/feedback. Lingual's defensibility should come from curriculum traceability, conversation design, teacher workflow, and pilot-to-report business process.

## School Market Baseline Expectations

The first-pass scan suggests that school buyers may expect the following in direct or near-direct competitors:

- Assignable speaking or conversation tasks.
- Teacher dashboard and class-level progress view.
- Student feedback immediately after practice.
- Teacher-visible transcripts, recordings, scores, or debriefs.
- Roster/class setup by manual import, Google Classroom, Clever, ClassLink, or LMS.
- Privacy and minor-data posture, especially FERPA/COPPA in the United States.
- Standards/proficiency alignment language such as WIDA, ACTFL, CEFR, ELD, or local curriculum objectives.
- Quote, school license, PO/invoice, or district purchasing path.
- Pilot support, onboarding, and customer success language.

Lingual does not need to match every expectation before first pilots, but sales messaging should avoid implying enterprise readiness before these surfaces are real.
