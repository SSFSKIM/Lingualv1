# Product Tear-Down Deep Dives

Status: Research draft
Last updated: 2026-06-01

## Purpose

This document is the evidence companion to
[15-product-teardown-v1.md](./15-product-teardown-v1.md).

The v1 teardown summarizes the market. This document captures deeper,
competitor-by-competitor product findings from public product pages, help
centers, public manuals, demos, studies, and school-facing pages.

The research lens is intentionally practical:

1. How does a teacher create or assign practice?
2. How natural is the student conversation experience?
3. What feedback or debrief does the product generate?
4. Does the teacher dashboard support class decisions?
5. What does this imply for Lingual's product and positioning?

## Executive Read

The strongest competitors are not simple AI speaking apps. They already cover
many school workflow basics: assignment setup, student practice, feedback,
teacher dashboards, rubrics, reporting, LMS or roster integration, and
procurement language.

The open gap is narrower and more valuable:

> Lingual should become the conversation-native, curriculum-traceable layer
> that turns student speaking practice into teacher-ready instructional
> evidence.

Market-level implications:

- In Korea, competitors are strongest around public-sector legitimacy,
  textbook/platform workflows, process assessment, and evidence artifacts.
- In the US, competitors are strongest around teacher workflows, rubrics,
  LMS/rostering, gradebook surfaces, and procurement readiness.
- Pronunciation scoring is table stakes. It should support the product, not
  define the product.
- "AI conversation exists" is not enough. Speakology, Speakable, ELSA, Nualang,
  and Telo all claim or show some version of AI conversation or roleplay.
- The stronger Lingual wedge is objective-linked conversation, student debrief,
  teacher evidence, class-level insight, and pilot reporting.

## Korea Deep Dives

### Plang School

Positioning:

Plang School is not positioned mainly as a free AI conversation partner. It is
positioned as a school workflow platform for AI-assisted English classwork and
process-centered assessment across writing, reading, speaking, and listening.
Its strongest school message is teacher control plus AI feedback.

Visible workflow:

- Teachers can create speaking and reading activities from textbook scripts,
  repeated expressions, student-written text, teacher-entered sentences, or
  pre-registered speaking topics.
- Public cases show reading-aloud activities, performance speaking topics,
  reading passages with AI-generated questions, and writing activities with
  rubrics or required expressions.
- Teacher dashboards identify students who are stuck, low-scoring, active,
  off-task, or distributed across achievement levels.

Student conversation UX:

The public school workflow appears mixed and teacher-directed. Speaking includes
reading aloud, repeated recording, pronunciation/fluency scoring, one-minute
speeches, and some roleplay after practice. Public evidence does not prove a
sustained open-ended AI conversation loop.

Feedback and dashboard:

Plang is strong on feedback and school evidence. Public materials describe AI
feedback on pronunciation, fluency, content, structure, vocabulary, and grammar,
with teacher review or supplemental feedback where needed. Plang also appears
strong around student-record-style activity summaries, which matters in Korean
school contexts.

Lingual implication:

Plang is the Korea product to beat on school vocabulary and assessment
evidence. Lingual should not copy Plang as a broad four-skill platform. Lingual
should be more conversation-native: teacher-designed conversation objectives,
natural multi-turn roleplay, transcript/debrief evidence, class-level objective
mastery, and "what should I reteach tomorrow?" reporting.

Need to be resolved:

- Exact logged-in assignment builder screens.
- Due dates, roster sync, rubric editing depth, and assignment reuse.
- Whether Plang School supports true open-ended multi-turn conversation.
- Privacy/compliance documentation and admin analytics depth.
- Whether Plang School supports languages beyond English.

### LG CNS Speaking Class / AI Digital Class

Positioning:

LG CNS is best read as an institutional platform competitor, not just a single
app competitor. Speaking Class, Buttertime, AI Digital Class, CMS/LMS, and
digital-textbook platform messaging all point to a broader education-DX sales
motion.

Visible workflow:

- Earlier Speaking Class materials describe teachers creating classes and
  content through a Studio workflow.
- Teachers can input English dialogues and have AI turn them into practice
  formats such as speaking, fill-in-the-blank, chunked speaking, and
  similar-answer practice.
- Current LG CNS education pages emphasize AI speaking, dashboards, learning
  history, GPT free conversation, prompt/persona management, and white-label
  delivery.

Student conversation UX:

The visible UX is mixed. Speaking Class appears more scaffolded and
model-answer-centered. Buttertime is more conversational, with situation
simulation, hints, and AI continuing the dialogue. Public evidence does not
prove real classroom naturalness, latency, or sustained open-ended turn-taking.

Feedback and dashboard:

Student feedback includes pronunciation, grammar accuracy, speech speed, answer
similarity, fluency, and personalized feedback. Teacher-facing claims include
progress, level, completion, weakness analytics, individual reports, dashboards,
and recommended/personalized content. Public dashboard screenshots remain thin.

Lingual implication:

LG CNS validates teacher-created content and institutional trust as table
stakes in Korea. Lingual should compete with a clearer teacher workflow and a
more defensible speaking evidence chain: objective-linked assignments,
open-ended but bounded roleplays, transcripts, debriefs, and class-level
misconception or skill heatmaps.

Need to be resolved:

- Current status of Speaking Class Studio.
- Whether AI Digital Class has replaced Speaking Class in active school sales.
- Actual teacher dashboard screens.
- Assignment due dates, rubric controls, roster/LMS integrations, and exports.
- School privacy terms and moderation/safety controls.

### EBS AI PengTalk

Positioning:

EBS AI PengTalk is the Korean public-sector baseline for AI English speaking
practice. It is free, elementary-school oriented, curriculum/textbook aligned,
EBS-backed, and connected to Ministry of Education infrastructure. It is closer
to a public learning utility than a private school SaaS product.

Visible workflow:

- Teachers request school member codes through the teacher page by entering
  school year, grade/class, and student count.
- LCMS admins issue codes and map students to teachers/classes.
- SchoolTalk is the clearest teacher-configured activity: teachers create a
  room, choose a topic, name the room, create groups, add students, track
  state, review conversations, and send feedback.

Student conversation UX:

The UX is mixed and bounded. TopicWorld is scripted practice. Speaking is
situation-based dialogue practice. Let's Talk is more conversational, where
students talk with Pengsoo around an episode topic and complete missions.
SchoolTalk is topic-based group discussion. Public evidence supports AI
speaking practice, but not fully open-ended tutoring.

Feedback and dashboard:

Student feedback includes pronunciation scoring, waveform comparison, recording
replay, stars, correctness bands, mission success, activity scores, attendance,
weekly learning time, and category evaluation graphs. Teachers can see student
codes, averages, accumulated activity, last study date, learning time,
attendance, completion by menu, rankings, and SchoolTalk review/feedback. LCMS
admins can access school/grade/class usage statistics and Excel downloads.

Lingual implication:

Do not compete with PengTalk as "free gamified English practice." Compete on
teacher control, custom curriculum, open-ended conversation, transcript-level
debrief, class-level recommendations, multi-language support, and school-ready
B2B packaging.

Need to be resolved:

- Current login-gated teacher dashboard details.
- Whether non-SchoolTalk activities can be assigned with deadlines.
- Whether teachers can create custom prompts or rubrics.
- Current exports beyond LCMS usage statistics.
- Current 2025-2026 teacher training walkthroughs.

### YBM / Visang AIDT

Positioning:

Visang is the more transparent public evidence source. It positions AIDT as a
full class platform connecting diagnosis, analysis, content, feedback,
AI-personalized learning, teacher reports, and training. YBM is more gated at
the screen-level product detail, but public pages position YBM AIDT around
learning analytics, adaptive learning, Y-Cloud, AI Writing, AI StoryChat,
English games, and phonics.

Visible workflow:

- Visang manuals show a clear homework/evaluation creation flow: teachers can
  load existing materials, use AI generation, or directly author content.
- Teachers configure name, date window, timer, automatic report sharing, and AI
  tutor availability.
- For AI-personalized learning, teachers select students, choose in-class or
  homework mode, set period, choose personalized or same-problem delivery, and
  issue work.
- Recording activities can be authored from unit vocabulary, sentences, and
  passages with pronunciation dimensions such as intonation, pause, stress,
  rhythm, and accuracy.

Student conversation UX:

Visang appears mostly structured: pronunciation drills, recording tasks, AI
Speaking, AI Writing, and AI Tutor Q&A. AI Speaking includes recording up to
five minutes, reviewing/editing speech-to-text, and receiving grammar/vocabulary
correction. YBM has more public "conversation" signals through AI StoryChat and
AI native-speaker teacher language, but exact current AIDT speaking screens are
not publicly proven.

Feedback and dashboard:

Visang is strong. Teacher reports include class average correctness, top
difficult items, students needing guidance, item/content stats, answer review,
teacher feedback, self/peer evaluation, report sharing, AI-generated teacher
comments, and learning maps. YBM claims diagnosis, dashboards, AI tutor,
assistant-teacher support, content recommendation, AI Writing scoring, and
slow-learner care, but screen-level evidence is weaker.

Lingual implication:

Do not pitch Lingual as another digital textbook. Pitch it as the speaking
practice layer that can sit alongside AIDT: AIDT manages textbook/courseware
workflow; Lingual produces authentic spoken performance evidence, natural
conversation, and teacher-ready debriefs.

Need to be resolved:

- YBM exact English/speaking assignment screens.
- YBM speaking feedback rubric and AI StoryChat voice/text modality.
- Whether either product supports teacher-created open-ended roleplays.
- Guardrails, privacy terms, and admin analytics beyond teacher/class
  dashboards.

## United States / Global Deep Dives

### Speakable

Positioning:

Speakable is an assignment-first AI speaking and writing platform for K-12 ELL
and World Language programs. It positions around more speaking/writing reps
without teacher grading overload, plus AP/IB/AAPPL/ACCESS/Seal of Biliteracy
prep, standards tracking, LMS workflows, privacy compliance, and admin-visible
data.

Visible workflow:

- Teachers can create AI-assisted activities by selecting language, standards,
  proficiency level, and learning focus.
- AI Studio and in-builder AI Chat can generate pages, add media, change grading
  methods, generate rubrics, and bulk-edit activities.
- Teachers publish, assign to classrooms, choose Practice or Assessment mode,
  set points, and optionally set time limits or browser lock.
- Google Classroom and LTI workflows are documented, including grade sync and
  LMS access on organization plans.

Student conversation UX:

Speakable is mixed. It supports repeat cards, written responses, open spoken
responses, and newer simulated conversations. Simulated Conversations are close
to Lingual's core: teachers configure topic, goals, level, AI voice/accent,
spoken or typed mode, attempts, hints, AI guidance, starting message, and
rubric. Naturalness is plausible but not fully proven by public full-session
recordings.

Feedback and dashboard:

Speakable is strong on feedback. It supports manual, pass/fail, rubric, and
proficiency/standards-based grading. Practice Mode can show immediate feedback.
Teacher review includes board/matrix/list views, audio playback, transcripts,
grammar insights, score details, feedback, chat history, comments, manual score
adjustment, full credit, and reopening for redo.

Lingual implication:

Speakable is the US benchmark for school-ready workflow. Lingual should meet
enough of the review/reporting bar, but differentiate with deeper live
conversation, stronger curriculum objective tracing, and pilot reporting that
shows teacher-useful evidence rather than only activity completion.

Need to be resolved:

- Full naturalness and latency of simulated conversation.
- Exact cohort filters, export formats, item analysis, and recommendation depth.
- Whether class-level reporting is prescriptive or mostly descriptive.

### Speakology AI

Positioning:

Speakology AI is the closest visible AI conversation/oral exam competitor. It
positions as an institution-ready platform for World Language and ESL programs:
AI-teacher video calls, oral quizzes/exams, automated grading, gradebook,
Canvas availability, standards alignment, and privacy/procurement readiness.
Yale's public Canvas tool page and Arabic case study are strong external
validation.

Visible workflow:

- Teachers can create courses, choose templates, generate access codes, create
  custom conversation topics, and create oral quizzes.
- Public UI evidence shows fields for topic, ACTFL level, intro prompt,
  vocabulary, verb tense focus, example questions, optional image, publish
  toggle, corrections, and feedback settings.
- Oral quiz creation includes intro/closing messages, retake controls,
  draft/publish, and questions.

Student conversation UX:

The UX is mixed but conversation-centered: guided AI conversation, open-ended
"talk about anything," roleplay/theme-based practice, and oral quiz/exam.
Students use a video-call-like interface with avatars, optional video,
audio-only/video modes, captions, speaking-speed adjustment, and translator
support. Naturalness is strongly claimed but independent latency/quality proof
is still limited.

Feedback and dashboard:

Students receive overall score, vocabulary used, highlighted grammar, rubric
categories, pronunciation/task completion feedback, ACTFL level, and AI
feedback. Teachers can review transcripts with timestamps, audio recordings,
attempts, scores, and feedback. Public evidence supports a real gradebook with
completion, quiz scores, assignment-by-student grids, transcripts, time spent,
attempts, audio playback, and course switching.

Lingual implication:

Lingual cannot differentiate simply by saying "AI conversation." Speakology
already does this. Lingual's wedge must be curriculum intelligence: diagnostic
placement, objective-level assignments, teacher-visible evidence, class-level
next actions, and stronger colloquial/pragmatic conversation quality.

Need to be resolved:

- Exact LMS technical model, roster sync, due dates, and grade passback.
- Rubric upload UI and score override workflow.
- Standards-level reporting, admin analytics, and scoring reliability.

### Telo AI

Positioning:

Telo positions as school-ready ELL/ML speaking-time infrastructure. The product
includes a classroom robot and a browser-based platform, both tied to a Teacher
Dashboard. Its wedge is clear: give English learners more one-on-one speaking
practice without adding staff, then convert conversations into teacher
insights.

Visible workflow:

Public evidence is strongest for student-facing conversation and procurement,
and weakest for actual teacher assignment creation. Telo says teachers choose
the right mode and that sessions can start from prompts such as chapter
vocabulary, quiz/review, or roleplay. The homepage says no uploads or
integration are required, suggesting a prompt-driven practice model rather than
a full teacher-authored assignment workflow.

Student conversation UX:

The UX appears mixed and fairly natural. Public demos show roleplay, targeted
speaking-skill practice, and content tutoring through a physical robot or web
interface. Roleplay examples include ordering food; targeted practice includes
expressing opinions with transition words and complete sentences. The web demo
uses a video-call-like interface with microphone/camera/chat controls and a
transcript/message panel. The physical robot creates a low-pressure classroom
experience. Some demos, however, look more like spoken tutoring than balanced
student speaking practice.

Feedback and dashboard:

In-session feedback includes validating complete sentences, prompting for
another reason, correcting misunderstandings, and scaffolding follow-up
answers. Claimed dashboard metrics include vocabulary growth, session length,
error types, engagement time, accuracy, conversation frequency, speaking
participation, language progression, and AI-generated insights. Public
materials claim alignment to WIDA, Texas ELPS, New York multilingual learner
frameworks, state ELPT standards, and California ELD standards.

Lingual implication:

Telo owns a strong "more speaking time for ELL students" message and a
robot-confidence story. Lingual should own curriculum-controlled conversational
practice: teacher creates a practice assignment from an objective, chooses
proficiency level and scenario, assigns it to a class, then sees transcript
evidence, rubric feedback, student talk time, error patterns, and class-level
next-step recommendations.

Need to be resolved:

- Assignment authoring, standards selection, due dates, class assignment, and
  rubric configuration.
- Exact student debrief and teacher summary format.
- Audio playback, transcript export, proficiency scoring, and retention
  controls.
- Dashboard screenshots, roster heatmaps, subgrouping, standards mastery,
  recommended mini-lessons, intervention flags, and admin reporting.
- Whether standards alignment is a real mapped report or marketing language.

### Extempore

Positioning:

Extempore is a school-grade language assessment platform, not primarily a
consumer language tutor. It owns teacher/admin-controlled oral assessment:
create prompts, collect audio/video/text/screen responses, score with rubrics,
leave feedback, export/report, and align departments.

Visible workflow:

- Teachers create assessments inside classes, name them, set start/due
  timelines, choose individual or group mode, set descriptions, grade-release
  mode, randomization, sequential mode, and assigned students.
- Question response types include video, audio, text, multiple choice,
  simulated conversation, and screen recording.
- Prompts can include text, media, YouTube, rubrics, timers, attempts, and
  constraints.
- LTI and LMS-integrated workflows connect Extempore assessments to external
  LMS tools.

Student conversation UX:

Extempore is mixed, but not a confirmed open-ended AI conversation product.
Individual tasks are asynchronous oral responses. Simulated conversation is
AP-style and fixed. Synchronous group assessments are live peer conversations
recorded for teacher review. The naturalness is strongest in peer sync rooms,
not AI tutoring.

Feedback and dashboard:

Teachers score with rubrics, leave written/audio/video feedback, publish grades,
unlock/reset attempts, and review transcripts. Gradebook supports filters,
color-coded statuses, editing, CSV export, recent activity, and grading flow.
Admin reports include date/assessment/campus/instructor/class filters, usage,
performance summaries, average scores, common assessment analysis, and exports.

Lingual implication:

Extempore defines the school assessment infrastructure bar. Lingual should not
compete as "we also record speaking." Lingual should combine enough assessment
reliability with a more natural adaptive AI conversation experience and
teacher-facing instructional signals.

Need to be resolved:

- Current general availability of AI scoring.
- District pricing and deeper security/DPA terms.
- Transcript support quality across languages.
- Whether reporting can recommend instructional next steps.

### Nualang

Positioning:

Nualang positions as a school-facing world-language practice platform with
teacher-created or publisher-linked content, scaffolded speaking/listening
practice, chatbot characters, student recordings/transcripts, and school
procurement through Wayside Publishing/Learning Site.

Visible workflow:

- Teachers create and manage courses, invite students by email or CSV, or use
  course codes and private enrollment keys.
- Roleplays can be created from scratch or templates by choosing language,
  name, description, difficulty, image, voice, public/private settings,
  dialogue, and comprehension questions.
- Chatbots can be hand-authored with triggers/responses or AI-generated from
  course context and class level, with teacher accept/edit/decline control.
- Discuss and Live support peer speaking and class games.

Student conversation UX:

The UX is mixed and scaffolded. Pronunciation drills score and allow retries.
Roleplays are pre-written or structured. Chatbots simulate AP-style
conversation where students type or speak. Discuss is peer-to-peer speaking
with prompt, recording, transcript, avatars, and backgrounds. It is more natural
than drills, but still bounded by teacher-selected content.

Feedback and dashboard:

Students get immediate corrective feedback, automatic pronunciation score,
playback, save/retry, and real-time chatbot nudges. Teachers get transcripts,
recordings, activity reports, responses, completion data, time spent, reports,
Excel downloads, printable reports, progress tabs, heat maps, and live score
overviews.

Lingual implication:

Nualang sets a high bar for teacher authoring and activity packaging. Lingual
should match the ease of creating scenarios while going deeper on realtime voice
conversation, curriculum objective tracing, diagnostic evidence, and next-step
teacher recommendations.

Need to be resolved:

- Exact gradebook passback.
- Discuss rubric categories and scoring model.
- Admin/district analytics depth.
- Whether newer AI chatbot feedback supersedes earlier safety language.

### ELSA Schools

Positioning:

ELSA Schools is an English speaking/pronunciation platform for K-12,
universities, and institutions. Its wedge is speech scoring depth:
pronunciation, intonation, fluency, grammar, vocabulary, CEFR/IELTS estimates,
and accent-aware recognition.

Visible workflow:

- Curriculum pages include content, ELSA content, and assignments.
- Assignments show start date, due date, average score, and completion rate.
- Public Read Aloud docs describe creating a lesson, choosing Read Aloud,
  pasting a passage, generating the lesson, reviewing warm-up vocabulary, and
  assigning to classes or saving to library.
- Older admin guides show study set creation, multi-class assignment, due
  dates, reminders, skills, lesson types, and review before assignment.

Student conversation UX:

ELSA is mixed, not purely open conversation. It includes scripted study sets and

appears strongest around assigned activities, scoring, and scenarios rather
than teacher-run oral exams.

Feedback and dashboard:

Students receive immediate quantitative and transcript-level feedback. ELSA
scores pronunciation, intonation, fluency, grammar, and vocabulary. Scripted

## Cross-Market Product Lessons

### 1. Teacher Authoring Must Be Concrete

The best competitors show actual creation flows: title, date window, class,
topic, target level, prompt, rubric, attempts, feedback mode, publishing, and
review. Lingual's assignment builder should make the teacher's control visible
in the first pilot demo.

### 2. Natural Conversation Must Be Proven, Not Claimed

Many competitors claim conversation, roleplay, or AI dialogue. Lingual needs a
demo that proves turn-taking, relevance, repair, target-expression use,
student comfort, and teacher control.

### 3. Feedback Needs Two Audiences

Students need a debrief they can act on. Teachers need evidence they can use to
plan class. Products that only produce a score or transcript are vulnerable to
Lingual if Lingual converts the conversation into instructional insight.

### 4. Dashboards Need To Say What To Do Next

Competitors often show completion, scores, time spent, and transcripts. Fewer
show clear next instructional moves. Lingual's dashboard should answer:

- Which students need attention?
- Which target objectives were weak?
- Which error patterns repeated across the class?
- Which follow-up assignment should be used next?
- What evidence can be included in a pilot or administrator report?

### 5. Korea And US Positioning Should Differ

Korea:

- Lead with teacher-controlled speaking evidence, process-assessment support,
  custom curriculum, and public-school-friendly reporting.
- Avoid positioning against free EBS-style practice directly.
- Do not pitch as another digital textbook.

US:

- Lead with school-safe AI speaking assignments, teacher review, transcripts,
  rubrics, LMS/roster readiness, and class-level evidence.
- Treat Speakable, Speakology, Extempore, and Nualang as workflow benchmarks.
- Make the pilot report a core differentiator.

## Recommended Lingual Product Responses

1. Build the teacher assignment builder around objective, scenario, target
   expressions, allowed support, feedback mode, and rubric.
2. Make every student conversation produce a transcript, student debrief, and
   teacher evidence card.
3. Add class-level summaries that identify repeated weaknesses and recommend a
   follow-up assignment.
4. Support teacher review/override before using AI feedback for assessment or
   formal records.
5. Package pilot reporting as a product output: speaking time, session length,
   completion/yield, satisfaction, sample debriefs, implementation friction,
   and class evidence.
6. Keep pronunciation scoring as a support signal, not the primary message.
7. Prepare school procurement basics early: privacy language, data deletion,
   teacher/admin roles, roster options, and eventually LMS/LTI.
