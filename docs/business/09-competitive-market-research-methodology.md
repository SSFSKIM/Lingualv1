# Competitive Market Research Methodology

Status: Business v1 draft
Last updated: 2026-06-01

## Purpose

This document defines how Lingual should research the competitive market before making claims about positioning, differentiation, pricing, or go-to-market strategy.

The research should focus on school-integrated B2B products for AI-powered conversational language learning, especially products where realtime conversation, speaking practice, or listening practice is central.

This is a methodology document only. It should not list competitor conclusions until source-based research is completed.

## Primary Research Question

What is the current market landscape for school- or institution-used AI-powered conversational language learning platforms, and where should Lingual position itself for Korea and the United States?

## Market Scope

### Korea

Primary market focus:

- Korean public elementary and middle schools.
- English-class adoption.
- District/government buyer path.
- AI-powered realtime conversational practice tools for English.

Secondary market focus:

- Multilingual school or institute tools.
- Private language institutes, if they reveal relevant product or pricing patterns.
- Government-facing education technology vendors, if they compete for the same buyer attention.

Research notes:

- The Korea public education purchase process still requires research.
- The research should separate product capability from procurement feasibility.
- A product can be pedagogically relevant but commercially irrelevant if it does not sell into schools or public education channels.

### United States

Primary market focus:

- ESL / ELD programs.
- Middle and high school language-learning programs.
- Public schools and district-facing products.
- School-integrated AI conversation, speaking, or listening practice platforms.

Secondary market focus:

- College language programs.
- Independent school world-language programs.
- Language lab replacements.
- LMS-integrated speaking practice or oral assessment tools.

Research notes:

- College should be treated as a later-phase market unless the product strongly overlaps with school B2B language practice.
- Consumer language apps should only be included when they are actively selling to schools, districts, institutions, or enterprise education buyers.

## Competitor Inclusion Criteria

Include a product if it matches at least two of the following:

- Built for schools, districts, colleges, language institutes, or education institutions.
- Uses AI to support conversation, speaking practice, listening practice, or oral interaction.
- Provides teacher, school, administrator, or institutional management surfaces.
- Supports classroom assignment, roster, LMS, reporting, or curriculum alignment workflows.
- Markets itself to B2B education buyers, not only individual learners.
- Offers evidence, analytics, reports, assessment support, or teacher-facing review of student practice.

## Competitor Exclusion Criteria

Exclude or demote products that are primarily:

- Consumer-only self-study language apps.
- Generic chatbots without school workflow.
- General tutoring platforms where language conversation is not a core use case.
- Pronunciation-only tools without broader conversational practice.
- Translation tools.
- LMS products without meaningful speaking/listening practice.
- Assessment-only tools with no practice workflow.

These products may still appear as adjacent substitutes, but they should not define the primary competitor set.

## Competitor Categories

### Direct Competitors

School- or institution-used AI-powered conversation practice platforms where speaking/listening practice is a central product promise.

### Near Competitors

Products that support AI language learning, speaking practice, language labs, or oral practice, but have weaker school workflow, weaker realtime conversation, or narrower use cases.

### Adjacent Substitutes

Products that buyers may compare against even if the product is not directly equivalent:

- Language lab platforms.
- LMS add-ons for speaking assignments.
- Oral assessment platforms.
- AI tutor products.
- Consumer language apps with school offerings.
- Teacher-created manual workflows using existing LMS, video, audio, or conferencing tools.

## Research Dimensions

Each product should be evaluated across the same dimensions:

| Dimension | What to capture |
| --- | --- |
| Company / product | Name, website, country, founding context if relevant. |
| Market | Korea, United States, global, or other. |
| Buyer | Teacher, department chair, school administrator, district/government, language institute, college. |
| Target segment | Elementary, middle, high school, ESL/ELD, world language, college, adult institute. |
| Core promise | What customer problem the product claims to solve. |
| Conversation mode | Realtime voice, async voice, text chat, roleplay, listening, pronunciation, assessment. |
| AI role | Conversation partner, feedback generator, scoring engine, tutor, content generator, report generator. |
| School workflow | Assignments, classes, rosters, admin roles, consent, reporting, curriculum alignment. |
| LMS/integration | Canvas, Google Classroom, Clever, ClassLink, LTI, SIS, SSO, manual setup. |
| Teacher control | Can teachers define goals, scenarios, rubrics, target expressions, curriculum, or limits? |
| Analytics/reporting | Speaking time, completion, rubric evidence, debriefs, admin dashboards, exports. |
| Compliance posture | Public claims around privacy, minors, data retention, FERPA/COPPA, Korea public education requirements. |
| Pricing/packaging | Free trial, pilot, per-student, per-teacher, school license, district license, custom pricing. |
| Sales motion | Self-serve, school pilot, institution sales, district/government procurement, partner channel. |
| Evidence strength | Official product page, docs, pricing page, case study, procurement listing, customer quote, news, third-party review. |
| Lingual implication | Threat, differentiation opportunity, partnership possibility, or research-only reference. |

## Evidence Quality Rules

Use source quality labels in the research output:

| Evidence label | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Official | Product website, documentation, pricing page, official blog, official case study. |
| Institutional | School, district, government, procurement, or university page mentioning usage or purchase. |
| Third-party | News, market report, review site, app marketplace, conference listing. |
| Inferred | Reasonable conclusion from multiple signals, but not explicitly stated by a source. |
| Need to be resolved | Important detail not available from current sources. |

Research should not treat marketing copy as proof of adoption, compliance, or procurement readiness. Adoption claims should be stronger when supported by institutional or procurement-facing sources.

## Research Process

### Phase 1: Broad Market Scan

Goal: build a longlist of candidate products.

Outputs:

- Korea candidate list.
- United States candidate list.
- Global products that actively sell into schools or institutions.
- Excluded products with reason for exclusion.

Search should include:

- English and Korean search terms.
- Product websites.
- school/district/customer pages.
- procurement or public-sector references.
- education technology directories.
- app marketplaces only as secondary evidence.

### Phase 2: Competitor Qualification

Goal: reduce the longlist to meaningful direct, near, and adjacent competitors.

For each candidate, decide:

- Direct competitor.
- Near competitor.
- Adjacent substitute.
- Excluded.
- Need to be resolved.

Qualification should be based on buyer, school workflow, AI conversation depth, and speaking/listening practice centrality.

### Phase 3: Competitor Profiles

Goal: produce short, source-backed profiles for the most relevant products.

Each profile should include:

- One-line description.
- Target buyer and segment.
- Core product promise.
- Conversation/speaking/listening capability.
- School workflow depth.
- Pricing/packaging signal.
- Evidence quality.
- Implication for Lingual.

### Phase 4: Market Map

Goal: turn product facts into positioning.

The market map should separate:

- Direct AI conversation-practice platforms for schools.
- Language lab / speaking assignment tools.
- Oral assessment and scoring tools.
- AI tutor or chatbot products.
- Consumer apps with institutional offerings.
- Manual or incumbent substitutes.

### Phase 5: Lingual Positioning Synthesis

Goal: define how Lingual should speak about itself.

The synthesis should answer:

- What category should Lingual claim?
- What buyer problem is most under-served?
- Which competitor claims should Lingual avoid copying?
- Which differentiators are credible now?
- Which differentiators require product work before sales can use them?
- Which markets should Lingual enter first: Korea public schools, US public schools, independent schools, ESL/ELD, language institutes, or college?

## Scoring Rubric

Use a 1-5 score for each product on:

| Score area | 1 | 5 |
| --- | --- | --- |
| School B2B fit | Consumer-only | Clearly built for school/institution buyers |
| Conversation depth | Static exercises | Realtime or rich AI conversation practice |
| Speaking/listening centrality | Peripheral | Core product promise |
| Teacher control | Little/no control | Teacher can shape goals, content, rubrics, and practice context |
| Workflow integration | Standalone | Classes, assignments, rosters, LMS/admin workflows |
| Reporting value | Basic completion | Teacher/school-useful learning evidence |
| Procurement relevance | No institutional path | Clear school, district, government, or institutional sales path |
| Threat level to Lingual | Low overlap | Strong overlap with Lingual's target buyer and value proposition |

Scores are research aids, not final truth. The written rationale matters more than the number.

## Required Research Outputs

The first research round should produce:

1. Korea competitor longlist.
2. United States competitor longlist.
3. Qualified competitor matrix.
4. Top direct competitor profiles.
5. Adjacent substitute map.
6. Lingual positioning implications.
7. Open questions for pricing, procurement, legal, and product roadmap.

## Non-Goals

This research should not yet:

- Decide final pricing.
- Make legal compliance claims.
- Produce sales copy without source-backed positioning.
- Treat consumer app popularity as school-market proof.
- Treat a product as a direct competitor only because it uses AI.
- Resolve Korea public education procurement without dedicated legal/procurement research.

## Open Questions Before Research Starts

- Which Korean search terms and education procurement sources should be considered mandatory?
- Deep profiles should be created as needed based on competitor relevance, not a fixed quota.
- College products should be included in the first research round, but separated from K-12 and ESL/ELD findings.
- Pricing should be collected when public; sales-contact, quote-based, or custom-pricing signals should be tracked separately.
- Public-school usage does not require official/institutional evidence for inclusion in the scan, but evidence strength should still be labeled clearly.
